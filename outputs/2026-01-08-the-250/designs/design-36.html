The Efficacy of Nature-Inspired Interventions in Reducing Patient Anxiety: A Randomized Controlled Trial in Healthcare Settings

Elena Rodriguez1,2, Michael Chen1, Sarah Williams3, James O'Connor2
1Department of Health Psychology, University of Urban Studies, New York, NY, USA 2Center for Healthcare Innovation, Metropolitan Hospital System, New York, NY, USA 3Institute for Environmental Medicine, Green Valley University, Portland, OR, USA
❦ ❦ ❦

Abstract

Background: Patient anxiety in healthcare settings remains a persistent challenge, affecting treatment outcomes and patient satisfaction. Recent evidence suggests that biophilic design elements and nature-inspired interventions may reduce psychological distress. However, rigorous controlled studies examining specific mechanisms remain limited.

Methods: We conducted a randomized controlled trial with 240 patients (age 18-75) across three hospital departments. Participants were randomly assigned to one of three conditions: nature-inspired visual interventions (n=80), nature sounds (n=80), or standard care control (n=80). Anxiety levels were measured using the State-Trait Anxiety Inventory (STAI) at baseline, 30 minutes post-intervention, and 24 hours post-intervention.

Results: Both intervention groups showed statistically significant reductions in state anxiety compared to controls (p<0.001). Visual interventions reduced STAI scores by 23.4% (95% CI: 18.2-28.6%), while auditory interventions showed 19.7% reduction (95% CI: 14.9-24.5%). Effects persisted at 24-hour follow-up for visual interventions (p=0.012) but not auditory interventions (p=0.089).

Conclusions: Nature-inspired interventions, particularly visual elements, offer a practical, low-cost approach to reducing patient anxiety in healthcare settings. These findings support the integration of biophilic design principles in hospital environments and suggest potential mechanisms for future investigation.

Introduction

Healthcare environments are inherently stressful for patients, with anxiety affecting approximately 60-80% of individuals seeking medical care [1]. This anxiety can manifest as increased pain perception, delayed recovery, reduced treatment adherence, and diminished overall satisfaction with care [2,3]. Traditional approaches to managing patient anxiety, including pharmacological interventions and psychological counseling, while effective, carry limitations in terms of cost, accessibility, and potential side effects [4].

The biophilia hypothesis, introduced by Wilson [5] and expanded by Kellert and Wilson [6], proposes that humans possess an innate tendency to seek connections with nature and other forms of life. This theoretical framework has inspired extensive research into biophilic design—the incorporation of natural elements into built environments to enhance human wellbeing [7]. Studies have demonstrated that exposure to natural environments or nature-inspired elements can reduce stress markers, lower blood pressure, and improve mood [8,9].

Nature-Based Interventions in Healthcare

Previous research has examined various nature-based interventions in healthcare contexts, including window views of natural landscapes [10], indoor plants [11], nature imagery [12], and natural sounds [13]. Ulrich's seminal study found that surgical patients with window views of trees had shorter postoperative hospital stays and required less pain medication compared to those viewing brick walls [10]. Similarly, Diette et al. demonstrated that nature scenes and sounds reduced pain during bronchoscopy procedures [14].

Despite these promising findings, methodological limitations persist in the literature. Many studies employ quasi-experimental designs, small sample sizes, or fail to control for confounding variables [15]. Furthermore, few studies have directly compared different modalities of nature-based interventions (visual versus auditory) or examined the persistence of effects beyond immediate post-intervention periods.

Study Objectives

The primary objective of this randomized controlled trial was to evaluate the efficacy of nature-inspired interventions—specifically visual and auditory modalities—in reducing patient anxiety in hospital settings. Secondary objectives included comparing the relative effectiveness of visual versus auditory interventions and assessing the duration of anxiolytic effects. We hypothesized that both intervention types would significantly reduce patient anxiety compared to standard care, with visual interventions demonstrating superior and more persistent effects based on attention restoration theory [16].

Methods

Study Design and Participants

This single-blind, three-arm randomized controlled trial was conducted between March 2024 and November 2024 at Metropolitan Hospital System, a large urban medical center in New York. The study protocol was approved by the Institutional Review Board (Protocol #2024-HS-1847) and registered with ClinicalTrials.gov (NCT05428391). All participants provided written informed consent prior to enrollment.

Eligible participants were adult patients (aged 18-75 years) scheduled for outpatient procedures or admitted to general medicine, surgical, or oncology departments. Inclusion criteria required baseline State-Trait Anxiety Inventory (STAI) state scores of 40 or higher, indicating moderate to high anxiety. Exclusion criteria included: severe cognitive impairment, active psychosis, severe visual or hearing impairments, current use of anxiolytic medications, or participation in other anxiety-reduction interventions within the previous 30 days.

Randomization and Blinding

Participants were randomly assigned in a 1:1:1 ratio to one of three groups using computer-generated random number sequences with variable block sizes (4-8) stratified by department. Allocation concealment was maintained using sequentially numbered, opaque sealed envelopes. Research staff conducting outcome assessments were blinded to group allocation. Due to the nature of the interventions, participants and intervention administrators could not be blinded.

Interventions

All interventions were administered in private patient rooms for 30 minutes. The visual intervention group viewed a curated collection of high-resolution nature imagery displayed on a 32-inch screen, including forest landscapes, flowing water, meadows, and botanical close-ups. Images cycled every 2 minutes with gentle transitions, accompanied by neutral ambient sound to control for auditory environment.

The auditory intervention group listened through noise-canceling headphones to recordings of natural soundscapes (birdsong, flowing streams, gentle rainfall, and forest ambiance) while viewing a neutral gray screen. The control group received standard care without additional interventions, maintaining typical room conditions.

Outcome Measures

The primary outcome was change in state anxiety assessed using the State-Trait Anxiety Inventory (STAI-S) [17], a validated 20-item self-report questionnaire with scores ranging from 20 to 80. Higher scores indicate greater anxiety levels. The STAI-S demonstrates excellent internal consistency (Cronbach's α = 0.89-0.92) and test-retest reliability.

Secondary outcomes included physiological measures (heart rate and blood pressure), patient satisfaction ratings, and qualitative feedback. Assessments were conducted at baseline (T0), immediately post-intervention (T1, 30 minutes), and at 24-hour follow-up (T2).

Statistical Analysis

Sample size calculation determined that 72 participants per group would provide 80% power to detect a clinically meaningful difference of 5 points in STAI-S scores (standard deviation = 10) with α = 0.05, accounting for 10% attrition. Data analysis followed intention-to-treat principles using R version 4.3.1.

Between-group differences in STAI-S scores were analyzed using linear mixed-effects models with time, intervention group, and their interaction as fixed effects, and participant as a random effect. Post-hoc pairwise comparisons employed Tukey's method for multiple comparison adjustment. Statistical significance was set at p < 0.05 (two-tailed).

Results

Participant Characteristics

A total of 312 patients were screened for eligibility, with 240 meeting inclusion criteria and completing randomization (Figure 1). The sample consisted of 142 women (59.2%) and 98 men (40.8%), with a mean age of 48.3 years (SD = 14.7). Baseline characteristics were well-balanced across groups (Table 1). Study retention was high, with 232 participants (96.7%) completing the 24-hour follow-up assessment.

Primary Outcome: State Anxiety

Linear mixed-effects models revealed significant time × group interaction effects (F(4,465) = 18.73, p < 0.001), indicating differential changes in anxiety across groups over time. At post-intervention (T1), both visual and auditory intervention groups demonstrated significantly lower STAI-S scores compared to controls (p < 0.001 for both comparisons). The visual intervention group showed a mean reduction of 12.3 points (95% CI: 10.1-14.5), representing a 23.4% decrease from baseline. The auditory intervention group exhibited a mean reduction of 10.2 points (95% CI: 8.2-12.2), corresponding to a 19.7% decrease. The control group showed minimal change (-1.8 points, p = 0.094).

At 24-hour follow-up (T2), the visual intervention group maintained significant anxiety reduction compared to baseline (mean difference = -6.8 points, 95% CI: -4.2 to -9.4, p = 0.012), while the auditory intervention group's benefits had largely dissipated (mean difference = -2.9 points, 95% CI: -0.3 to -6.1, p = 0.089). Pairwise comparisons at T2 revealed that the visual intervention group had significantly lower anxiety than both auditory (p = 0.041) and control groups (p = 0.003).

Secondary Outcomes

Physiological measures corroborated self-report findings. Heart rate reductions paralleled STAI-S changes, with visual and auditory interventions showing mean decreases of 7.4 bpm (p < 0.001) and 5.8 bpm (p = 0.002) respectively at T1, compared to 1.2 bpm in controls (p = 0.341). Systolic blood pressure decreased by an average of 5.2 mmHg in the visual group (p = 0.008) and 3.8 mmHg in the auditory group (p = 0.029), versus 0.9 mmHg in controls (p = 0.518).

Patient satisfaction ratings were significantly higher in both intervention groups. On a 10-point scale, visual intervention participants rated their experience at 8.3 (SD = 1.4), auditory participants at 7.9 (SD = 1.6), and control participants at 6.1 (SD = 1.8). Qualitative feedback highlighted appreciation for "calming natural elements" and "distraction from medical environment," with several participants requesting continued access to interventions.

Discussion

This randomized controlled trial demonstrates that nature-inspired interventions—both visual and auditory—significantly reduce patient anxiety in healthcare settings. The magnitude of anxiety reduction observed (19.7-23.4%) exceeds clinically meaningful thresholds [18] and compares favorably to effects reported for brief psychological interventions [19]. Importantly, visual interventions produced more durable effects, maintaining significant anxiety reduction at 24-hour follow-up, suggesting potential for sustained therapeutic benefit.

Theoretical Implications

Our findings align with attention restoration theory (ART) [16], which posits that natural environments provide "soft fascination" that allows directed attention mechanisms to rest and recover. Visual nature stimuli may engage involuntary attention more effectively than auditory stimuli, explaining the superior persistence of visual intervention effects. The results also support stress reduction theory [20], which suggests that evolutionary-based responses to natural environments trigger parasympathetic nervous system activation, evidenced by our observed physiological changes.

The differential persistence of visual versus auditory effects warrants further investigation. Possible explanations include: (1) visual processing may create more enduring mental imagery that continues to provide anxiolytic effects post-intervention; (2) the dynamic nature of visual stimuli (changing images) may facilitate deeper engagement compared to continuous auditory streams; or (3) individual differences in sensory processing preferences may moderate intervention effectiveness.

Clinical Applications

The practical implications for healthcare environments are substantial. Nature-inspired visual interventions represent a low-cost, scalable approach to improving patient experience and outcomes. Implementation could include nature artwork, video displays in waiting areas and patient rooms, or window views where architecturally feasible [21]. Given the minimal resource requirements and absence of adverse effects, these interventions could be widely adopted across diverse healthcare settings.

Healthcare administrators and designers should consider biophilic principles as integral to facility planning rather than aesthetic add-ons. The sustained effects of visual interventions suggest that permanent environmental modifications (e.g., nature murals, botanical gardens, water features) may provide ongoing therapeutic value. Integration with existing anxiety management protocols could enhance comprehensive patient care.

Limitations and Future Directions

Several limitations merit consideration. First, the single-site urban hospital setting may limit generalizability to rural or community hospitals. Second, the 30-minute intervention duration, while practical, represents only one possible implementation model; dose-response relationships require investigation. Third, we did not assess potential moderators such as nature affinity, previous nature exposure, or cultural background, which may influence intervention effectiveness [22].

Future research should examine longer-term outcomes, including impacts on treatment adherence, recovery trajectories, and healthcare utilization. Neuroimaging studies could elucidate neural mechanisms underlying anxiolytic effects. Additionally, comparative effectiveness research examining specific elements of nature imagery (e.g., water versus forests, static versus dynamic presentations) could optimize intervention design. Investigating the role of personal nature connection and cultural factors in moderating intervention effects would advance personalized environmental medicine approaches.

Conclusions

This randomized controlled trial provides robust evidence that nature-inspired interventions, particularly visual modalities, effectively reduce patient anxiety in healthcare settings with effects persisting beyond immediate exposure. These findings support the integration of biophilic design principles into healthcare environments as an evidence-based strategy for enhancing patient wellbeing. As healthcare systems increasingly recognize the importance of holistic, patient-centered care, nature-based interventions offer a practical, accessible approach to addressing the pervasive challenge of patient anxiety while honoring humanity's innate connection to the natural world.
